Executive Summary

Problem: While NATO only recently formalized "cognitive warfare" (2023), Argentina's last military dictatorship (1976-1983) provides an earlier precedent through psychological operations under the U.S.-orchestrated Plan Condor. Today, these techniques are being redeployed in Argentine political discourse, weaponizing collective trauma and normalizing authoritarian ideologies.

Methodology: COGWAR REPORT integrates NLP, fine-tuned LLMs, and generative AI to create a multi-dimensional analysis framework that detects cognitive warfare tactics in political speeches. The project includes custom datasets from official sources (NATO, RAND, declassified intelligence) and develops speculative digital countermeasures.

Key Innovation: A comprehensive framework that analyzes emotional manipulation, binary thinking, geopolitical framing, and symbolic violence in Spanish-language political discourse, while exploring how different AI models (Meta vs. DeepSeek) produce divergent interpretations based on their geopolitical origins.

Critical Challenge: The ethical dilemma of releasing this as an OSINT tool versus the risk of enabling more sophisticated cognitive warfare attacks.

Technical Context & Motivation

The Psychological Nature of Deterrence

My research began with studying military deterrence theory: deterrence succeeds when an actor refrains from action due to internal conviction that they either 1) cannot win or 2) could win but consequences would be too destructive. The deterred actor loses without fighting, while their opponent wins without lifting a finger.

What fascinated me most about deterrence is its profoundly psychological character. Your enemy must believe your threats and power displays are real for deterrence to take effect. This psychological dimension becomes central to understanding cognitive warfare.

From Nuclear to Cognitive Domains

Post-Cold War, the U.S. shifted from nuclear unipolarity to a multipolar world with fragile alliances. Unable to maintain "First Strike Principle" advantages, military doctrine expanded to new domains‚Äîincluding the cognitive domain formalized by NATO in 2023.

Argentina's Vulnerability

Argentina faces a specific vulnerability: our unhealed wound from the 1976-1983 dictatorship. While we prosecuted military leaders, civilian collaborators were never held accountable. Today, political discourse weaponizes this trauma, reactivating fear of politics, fear of knowledge, and individualistic paranoia necessary for surviving genocidal dictatorship.

Plan C√≥ndor (Operation Condor)

A coordinated intelligence and repression operation that functioned between 1975-1983, implemented by the military dictatorships of the Southern Cone (Argentina, Chile, Uruguay, Paraguay, Bolivia, and Brazil) with logistical, financial, and doctrinal support, coordination, and direction from the United States.

Cognitive Warfare (NATO Definition, 2023)

Official NATO Definition: "The cognitive domain encompasses the mind and the way knowledge, understanding, and comprehension affect decision-making. Cognitive warfare seeks to change not only what people think, but also how they think and how they act."

Operational Characteristics (NATO Allied Command Transformation, 2023):

"Synchronized and coordinated attacks on emotions, thoughts, and behaviors affect will, morale, decision-making, and understanding of reality. Cognitive attacks are designed to use information to activate our brain's subconscious processes, making it difficult for our conscious mind to perceive the presence of a cognitive threat."

Key Distinction: Unlike information warfare (which targets what people think), cognitive warfare targets how people think, operating below conscious awareness to alter cognitive processes, emotional responses, and decision-making patterns.
